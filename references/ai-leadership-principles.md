# AI Leadership Principles — Omri at Linx

> Established: 2026-04-19
> Context: Dor assigned Omri to lead AI at Linx from Product on Apr 19. Omri is an experienced PM but not an AI expert; these principles define how to lead the mandate well anyway.
> Status: Living document. Challenge and revise.

---

## Foundation — why we're here

### 1. Value over velocity
Every AI feature passes one test: *"what customer pain does this solve, and would a customer pay more for it?"* If the answer isn't yes, we don't ship. Demo-worthy is not a shipping criterion.

### 2. Customer voice in every AI room
The PM reflex, dialed to 11. AI conversations skew fast toward what's technically interesting. We are the anchor that keeps every conversation on the customer.

---

## Craft — how we do the work

### 3. Evaluation is the lever
We measure AI output quality *before* we scale features. If we can't measure it, we can't lead it. This is where a non-AI-expert PM creates asymmetric value — judgment about "is this actually good?" beats model architecture every time. Sony's false confirmations would have been caught by an evals bar.

### 4. Reliability is the product
For AI features, under-engineering reliability is worse than over-engineering the UI. Accuracy toggles, fallbacks, confidence transparency, human-in-the-loop gates are *product*, not polish. Customers pay for trustworthy AI, not clever AI.

### 5. Own the failure-mode vocabulary
Hallucinations. False confirmations. Confidence collapse. Compound error. Context rot. We use precise language with engineering, customers, and leadership — not hand-waving. Mastering the taxonomy earns the right to lead the category.

### 6. Best practices, not bleeding edge
We follow patterns that other AI product teams have already shipped and validated — not ones we read about last week. Weekly reading cadence: Simon Willison, Hamel Husain, Shreya Shankar, Eugene Yan, Linus Lee, Chip Huyen. Practice, not research.

---

## Stance — how we lead and show up

### 7. "What we don't do" is a product bet
Saying no to seductive features is the discipline that compounds. Sony's accuracy toggle is a "no" to always-helpful behavior — that *is* our product bet. Make the "what we don't do" list visible to Dor, Niv, and customers.

### 8. Small wins earn trust — but don't become small thinking
We ship real, visible, customer-valued fixes to earn the right to lead. And we hold parallel ambition for buckets 2 (future customer AI) and 3 (company AI internal). 30-min weekly "what's the real ambition here?" block keeps the long arc alive.

### 9. Trust compounds slowly, collapses fast
Under-promise, over-deliver. Anything committed to Dor, Amir, or a customer ships — or we re-negotiate before the deadline. A single missed reliability promise on AI undoes months of goodwill.

### 10. Partner, don't pretend
Amir (Head of AI) and Shon (AI TL) are the experts. Our job is to translate between AI-engineering and customer/business/leadership, and to challenge with questions that force clear answers. We never fake depth we don't have. We never concede ground that's ours — product judgment, customer voice, evaluation discipline.

## How to use this document

- **Before any AI decision:** does this pass Principle 1 and 2?
- **Before any ship:** does this pass Principle 3, 4, 5?
- **Before any commitment to Dor/Amir/customer:** does Principle 9 hold?
- **Monthly review:** which principle did we uphold hardest? Which one did we slip on? Revise.
